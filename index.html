Methodology & Standards

Data Processing

  • Raw sensor data used directly — no resampling or interpolation applied
  • Weather data smoothed with 168-minute SG filter per EyeSense spec
  • Files with <2 weeks data excluded
  • Monitoring-only: avgTcs, avgC, avgP, avgE not used

Basic Metrics

  • Damp/Mould (BS 5250, ISO 13788): RH>70% sustained, RH>80% germination, dew point <3°C margin
  • Overheating (CIBSE TM59): >26°C for >3% occupied hours exceeds limit; 28°C corridor threshold
  • Under-heating (WHO/NICE): <18°C health risk, <16°C respiratory, <12°C cardiovascular
  • Ventilation (Part F): CO2 >1500ppm inadequate, >1000ppm poor
  • Fuel Poverty (Switchee HFPI): Never reaching 18°C in 7-day rolling window

Combined-Variable Metrics

  • VPX (BRE IP 1/06): eindoor - eoutdoor. >300Pa = excess moisture, >600Pa = intervention needed
  • CRI (ISO 13788): Tsurface - Tdewpoint, fRsi=0.75. CRI<1K = condensation, <3K = mould
  • EHR: Actual RH - expected RH
  • Adaptive Comfort (CIBSE TM52): Tmax=0.33×Trm+21.8+2
  • TRI: σ(indoor T)/σ(outdoor T)
  • NPE: Night purge effectiveness
  • Window Opening: Simultaneous dT<-0.5°C + dCO2<-50ppm + occupied + outdoor cooler
  • Wind-Infiltration: Wind speed vs cooling rate correlation
  • HDD (CIBSE TM41): Base 15.5°C
  • Solar Correlation: Pearson r (lux vs temp, 08-18h)

Standards

  • BS 5250:2021, BS EN ISO 13788, BRE IP 1/06
  • CIBSE TM52, TM59, Guide A, TM41
  • WHO Housing and Health Guidelines, NICE NG6
  • Building Regulations Part F, HHSRS, ASHRAE 160

Parameter Guide

This guide explains every parameter, metric, and rating used in this dashboard, what it means for the property, and how it is calculated from the raw sensor data.

Indoor Temperature (avgT)

Air temperature measured inside the property in degrees Celsius. The EyeSense sensor in the corridor and the SENS sensor in the room each record this independently at 5-minute intervals. This is the primary variable for assessing overheating and under-heating.

Indoor Relative Humidity (avgH)

Percentage of moisture in the air relative to the maximum the air can hold at that temperature. High RH (>70%) over extended periods creates conditions for mould growth. RH above 80% at surfaces is the germination threshold for most mould species. Comfortable range is 40-60%.

CO2 Concentration (avgCO2)

Carbon dioxide in parts per million. Outdoor baseline is ~420 ppm. Occupants exhale CO2, so rising levels indicate people are present and the space may not be adequately ventilated. Above 1000 ppm ventilation should be improved; above 1500 ppm is inadequate per Building Regulations Part F. CO2 is used as a proxy for ventilation effectiveness.

Light Intensity (avgLux)

Indoor light level in lux. Used as a proxy for solar gain — higher lux during daytime indicates more sunlight entering the property, which contributes to passive heating and potential overheating. The solar correlation metric (Pearson r between lux and temperature during 08:00-18:00) reveals how sensitive the property is to solar-driven temperature rises.

Occupancy Energy (avgRe)

Motion/occupancy energy from the PIR sensor. Values above 15 indicate the space is occupied. This is used to distinguish occupied from unoccupied periods for analysis — environmental conditions during occupation (higher CO2, humidity from breathing and activity) differ significantly from unoccupied periods.

Occupancy Fraction (avgrdr)

The fraction of the 5-minute sampling window during which occupancy was detected (0.0 to 1.0). Values above 0.3 are classified as occupied periods. This is used in the Occupancy Impact tab to split all environmental metrics into occupied vs unoccupied conditions, demonstrating how human presence affects the indoor environment.

Outdoor Weather Data

External weather conditions correlated by timestamp from a weather API: temperature (°C), humidity (%), wind speed (m/s), cloud cover (%), and precipitation (mm). These are essential for calculating combined metrics like VPX, CRI, and thermal responsiveness — they provide the outdoor baseline against which indoor conditions are compared.

Derived Metrics — Damp & Mould

Mould Risk Score (0-100)

Formula: 0.5 × (%time RH>70%) + 1.5 × (%time RH>80%) + 0.3 × (%time dew point within 3°C of air temp), capped at 100.
Ratings: Low (<10), Medium (10-20), High (20-40), Critical (>40).
Standards: BS 5250:2021 (sustained RH > 70% indicates risk), BS EN ISO 13788 (surface RH > 80% = mould germination).

Vapour Pressure Excess — VPX (Pa)

Formula: VPX = esat(Tindoor) × RHindoor/100 - esat(Toutdoor) × RHoutdoor/100, where esat(T) = 610.94 × exp(17.625T / (T+243.04)).
What it means: The difference between indoor and outdoor vapour pressure reveals excess moisture being generated inside. Normal (<200 Pa), Elevated (200-300), High (>300 — likely drying clothes, poor ventilation), Very High (>600 — intervention needed).
Standard: BRE Information Paper IP 1/06.

Condensation Risk Index — CRI (K)

Formula: CRI = Tsurface - Tdewpoint, where Tsurface = fRsi × (Tindoor - Toutdoor) + Toutdoor, using fRsi = 0.75 (worst thermal bridge).
What it means: Estimates how close the coldest surface in the property is to the dew point. CRI > 5K = Low risk, 3-5K = Moderate, 1-3K = High (mould likely), <1K = Critical (condensation occurring).
Standard: BS EN ISO 13788.

Excess Humidity Ratio — EHR (%RH)

Formula: EHR = Actual indoor RH - Expected indoor RH if outdoor air were heated to indoor temperature with no moisture added.
What it means: Isolates moisture generated inside the property from background outdoor moisture. EHR <5% = normal, 5-15% = moderate (normal occupancy), 15-25% = high (drying clothes, cooking), >25% = very high (water ingress or severely poor ventilation).

Derived Metrics — Temperature

Overheating Rating (CIBSE TM59)

Criterion: Indoor temperature exceeding 26°C for more than 3% of occupied hours exceeds CIBSE limits. 28°C is the severe/corridor threshold.
Ratings: Pass (≤1%), Warning (1-3%), Exceeds Limit (>3%).
Standard: CIBSE TM59 Design methodology for the assessment of overheating risk in homes.

Adaptive Comfort Exceedance (CIBSE TM52)

Formula: Upper comfort limit = 0.33 × Trunning mean outdoor + 21.8 + 2°C (Category II). Running mean: Trm = 0.8 × Trm,yesterday + 0.2 × Toutdoor,yesterday.
What it means: People adapt to warmer weather, so the acceptable indoor temperature rises with outdoor temperature. This metric shows the % of time indoor temperatures exceed this adaptive threshold.

Under-heating Rating (WHO)

Thresholds: Below 18°C = health risk for healthy adults. Below 16°C = respiratory risk. Below 12°C = cardiovascular stress and hypothermia risk. 20-21°C recommended for elderly/vulnerable.
Ratings: Low (≤5%), Medium (5-10%), High (10-20%), Critical (>20% of time below 18°C).
Standards: WHO Housing and Health Guidelines, NICE NG6.

Fuel Poverty Indicator (Switchee HFPI)

Criterion: Property flagged if it never reaches 18°C during any 7-day rolling window.
What it means: Indicates the resident may be unable to afford adequate heating, or the heating system is fundamentally inadequate. This is a strong indicator of fuel poverty and potential health risk.

Heating Degree Days — HDD

Formula: HDD = Σ max(0, 15.5 - Toutdoor) / 288 per 5-min reading.
What it means: Cumulative measure of how much heating is needed over the monitoring period. Base temperature 15.5°C (CIBSE TM41 UK standard). Higher HDD = colder period requiring more energy. Used to normalise energy usage comparisons between properties.

Derived Metrics — Ventilation & Behaviour

Ventilation Rating (Part F)

Thresholds: Mean CO2 <800 ppm = Good, 800-1000 = Acceptable, 1000-1500 = Poor, >1500 = Inadequate.
Standard: Building Regulations Approved Document Part F — average CO2 must not exceed 1500 ppm during occupied periods.

Window Opening Events

Detection: A window opening is identified when all four conditions occur simultaneously within a 5-minute step: indoor temperature drops >0.5°C, CO2 drops >50 ppm, the space is occupied (avgRe > 15), and outdoor temperature is cooler than indoor.
What it means: Indicates active ventilation behaviour by residents. Properties with very few window events and high CO2 may need ventilation improvements or resident engagement.

Derived Metrics — Building Fabric

Thermal Responsiveness Index — TRI

Formula: TRI = σ(indoor temperature) / σ(outdoor temperature).
What it means: How much the indoor temperature tracks outdoor temperature swings. TRI <0.3 = heavy thermal mass (concrete, thick masonry — temperature well-buffered). TRI >0.7 = lightweight construction (timber frame — temperature tracks outdoor changes closely). Lower TRI generally indicates better thermal performance.
Ratings: Heavy (<0.3), Moderate (0.3-0.7), Lightweight (>0.7).

Night Purge Effectiveness — NPE

Formula: NPE = (T22:00 - T06:00) / (T22:00 - Toutdoor,night mean).
What it means: How effectively the building cools overnight by releasing stored heat. NPE >0.8 = excellent (building cools nearly to outdoor temp), 0.5-0.8 = good, 0.2-0.5 = moderate (thermal mass retention), <0.2 = poor (sealed building, high internal heat retained).

Wind-Infiltration Correlation

Method: Pearson correlation between wind speed and indoor cooling rate (°C/hour) during unoccupied periods.
What it means: A strong negative correlation (e.g., r < -0.3) indicates the building loses heat faster when it is windy — a signature of air leakage through the building fabric. Properties with high wind-infiltration correlation would benefit from draught-proofing or airtightness improvements.

Solar-Temperature Correlation

Method: Pearson correlation between indoor lux and indoor temperature during daytime hours (08:00-18:00).
What it means: High positive correlation indicates the property is sensitive to solar gain — large glazing, south-facing, or low thermal mass. This is useful in summer to identify overheating risk from solar exposure.

Corridor vs Room Comparison

Delta (Δ) Values

The difference between the room sensor (lived-in space) and the corridor sensor (transitional space). Shown as Room minus Corridor.
ΔT (Temperature): Large positive delta = room is warmer (may indicate localised heating or solar gain). Large negative = room is colder (possible insulation issue). Highlighted amber if ≥2°C, red if ≥4°C.
ΔRH (Humidity): Higher room humidity suggests moisture generation from occupancy/activities. Highlighted amber if ≥5%, red if ≥10%.
ΔMould Score: Higher room mould score indicates the lived-in space has worse conditions than the corridor. Highlighted amber if ≥5 points, red with warning icon if ≥15 points.

Clustering

K-Means Clustering

Properties are grouped by similarity across 14 environmental features: mean temperature, temperature std, mean humidity, % high RH, % overheating, % under-heating, mean CO2, indoor-outdoor ΔT, temperature variability, occupancy %, mean VPX, mean CRI, mean EHR, and TRI. All features are min-max normalised. The optimal number of clusters (k) is selected by testing k=2 to 6 and choosing the highest Silhouette Score, which measures how well-separated the clusters are. Each cluster represents a group of properties with similar environmental behaviour patterns.
